Factors to consider when seeking to purchase a car

Drive wheel type
There are four different drive wheel types to choose from: front wheel drive (FWD), rear wheel drive (RWD), all-wheel-drive (AWD) and four wheel drive (4WD). The following table provides a description and lists the pros and cons of each drive wheel type.
Comparison of drive wheel types
Hidden Description Pros Cons
Front wheel drive (FWD) In a FWD drive vehicle, the two front wheels "push" the vehicle while the back two wheels just "ride" along. Fuel efficient, low-cost, great traction during inclement weather Not high performance, quite fragile
Rear wheel drive (RWD) In a RWD drive vehicle, the back two wheels "drive" the vehicle and also attempt to evenly distribute the weight of the car from back to front. Durable, easy handling, fast performance Poor traction
All wheel drive (AWD) In an AWD vehicle, all wheels receive various amounts of power all the time. This allows the vehicle to remain stable and extra power can be supplied to either set of wheels (front or rear) if needed. Excellent traction, Good for mild inclement weather, Able to adapt with changes in terrain More expensive than FWD and RWD, Maintenace costs are high, Heavy (less fuel-efficient)
Four wheel drive (4WD) In a 4WD vehicle, all wheels receive equal amounts of power, and most 4WD vechicles allow you to switch between two wheel drive (FWD and RWD) and four wheel drive. Provides the best traction for a vehicle, more fuel-efficient than AWD systems Adds much weight to vehicle, More expensive than RWD and FWD.
Fuel type
The two most common fuel types are gasoline (petrol) and diesel. Gasoline is used in many consumer vehicles; whereas, diesel is primarily used for buses, trains, and trucks. Although, some car manufacturers have started using diesel fuel for their cars, for example the 2016 Mercedes-Benz E-Class Sedan. Also, alternative fuels, such as ethanol and electricty, are gaining popularity.

Number of Cylinders
Cylinders are the fundamental parts of an engine. Most cars have either 4 (inline 4 or flat 4), 6 (V6), or 8 (V8) cylinders. Generally, cars with more cylinders are able to handle heavier loads and have better performance than cars with fewer cylinders.
Mileage
Mileage is not a problem if you are purchasing a new vehicle, but if you decide to purchase a used vehicle, you need to take into consideration mileage. Mileage is usually related to the the car's age. If possible, try to purchase a car with less than 110,000 miles and fewer than 10 years old because older cars can require much maintenance.
Miles per gallon (MPG)
The miles per gallon (mpg) provides an estimate of the number of miles a vehicle can travel on one gallon of fuel. MPG has two listings: city and highway. The city rating is usually lower than the highway listing since more fuel is consumed while driving in the city due to frequent stops; whereas, less fuel is consumed while driving on the highway since the vehicle travels at a constant speed.
Price
Perhaps the most important thing to consider when purchasing a car is price. The price of a car is dependent on many things such as the type/size of car (luxury, sedan, etc.), additional features, and the age of the vehicle.
Running costs
Commonly overlooked by many car buyers, running costs should be taken into consideration before purchasing a car. Running costs include depreciation, registration, insurance, fuel, taxes, and maintenance. For more information and examples please visit this website
Safety features
Cars are required to implement some safety features; while other car manufacturers often implement additional features in order to prove that their vehicles are the safest. Standard safety features include airbags, seatbelts, and antilock brakes. Some newer features include blind-spot warning, pedestrian detection, and automatic emergency braking. Transmission Types
There are four types of transmissions in cars: manual, automatic, semi-automatic, and continuously variable transmission (CVT). In the manual transmission, gears are changed by utilizing the gear shift and clutch pedal. The automatic transmission changes gears without the interaction of a human (usually done by the vehicle's computer). Semi-automatic transmissions utilize a gear shift, also known as simple shifter or paddles, to change gears but do not require a clutch pedal. In CVT transmissions, gears are not used to change speeds; instead, rubber or metal belts control the changing of speeds.
